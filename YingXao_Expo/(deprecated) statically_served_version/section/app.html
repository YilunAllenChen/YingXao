Why 3D Printing?

By Allen Chen

In the early days of YingXao, as we started to think about how we want to achieve the goals we set for the project, we realized very quickly that we need highly specialized hardware that are of irregular shapes. For example, our sunlight control mechanism required a special pair of spur gears. The follower gear needs to be not only hollow enough so it can sit comfortably on our base, but also bulky enough to hold up the blackout panel (See our preliminary design on the right).

Also, knowing that we have limited exposure to mechanical designs, we thought that we would probably need many iterations to have a robust functional design. This puts us on a tight schedule for each iteration. That means we need to, not only move fast, but also get results fast.

Having all these constraints, it seemed like 3D printing is our only option. Fast turnover rate, the ability to print all kinds of irregular shapes, and solid build quality (if you do it right).

Proof of Concept at Home, Production Build at Studio

By Allen Chen

Conveniently enough, Allen has a 3-D printer from AnyCubic in his bedroom. This is not a surprise becauase Allen is a huge advocate for rapid prototyping and maker culture. Although this printer doesn't have top-tier print quality, it still prints really solid builds if we judge on a proof-of-concept prototype standard.

The advantage of printing at home is that it has near-zero turnover time. During our peak times, we complete one iteration on a design every two days, and it stayed like that for two weeks. This allowed

us to have, within the first 2 months of development:

  • 3 iter. of Backpack (deprecated)
  • 3 iter. of Midplate (deprecated)
  • 4 iter. of Motor Mount (deprecated)
  • 6 iter. of Solar Gear Bearing
  • 13 iter. of Solar Gear Driver
  • 7 iter. of Solar Gear Ring
  • 5 iter. of Universal Mount

And whenever we are in need of production-level builds, we go to makerspaces that have high-end 3-D printers to print our parts.

Universal Mount: Centralized space for electronics

By Allen Chen


Figure: Preliminary design sketch of YingXao's Universal Mount

At the very beginning, we were thinking about placing our electronics and actuators (for sunlight and water control) in two separate places. And yes, you guessed it right, it was the deprecated "Backpack" and "Motor Mount" components. We made such a decision after realizing that there's really no benefit in putting these in a distributed manner. Pretty much the opposite, it gives several siginificant drawbacks: It openly exposes some of the most critical wires, introduces difficulties holding both of them in place when we try to put on the Solar Gear System, and a good number of other smaller problems.

Therefore we decided that we would put all electronics, and really anything that's sensitive at all, in a big box. And intuitively we would call it, the Universal Mount. The Universal Mount module clings onto our pot, and acts as the brain and hand of our system.

The Solar Gear System

By Allen Chen

Our team had a lot of interesting discussions on how we want to approach the sunlight control problem. What was obvious to us was that we need an acutation system, but how do we want to build it?

To figure that out, we first need to decide which way to go - sideways, around, or vertically? All of the three ways seemed viable to us as ways to control sunlight input for our system.

We decided to explore the vertical option, only to find that it was a dead end. We called it the "Toaster" approach, because when we lift a blind up to block the sunlight, the system looks like a toaster with a toast inside poking its head out. The success of this approach depended on two key factors, which we found both too difficult to solve in a clean way:

  1. We need to store the blind inside the toaster, just like the car window needs to be stored in the door. That means the toaster needs to be at least as tall, if not taller, than the blind.
  2. We need to maintain the stability of the blind once it's fully up without support from the sides.

We then turned to the "around" option, and decided to go all in with that. Not only is it going to be much more robust than the other two approaches, but it's also cool to look at. I mean, who doesn't like gears?

We will have a spur gear system, with the smaller driver gear being driven by our stepper motor. The bigger follower gear, which we call the Ring, will be held in place in terms of translation but free in terms of rotation. It will hold up a blocker that only covers a portion of the entire circle. We hold the Ring in place by letting it sit comfortably on a base, which we call the Bearing.

Months later, as we started doing hardware/software integration, we also added numerous minor tweaks to make them comply to our system requirements. Namely:

  • Completely fill one of the gap between two Ring's gear teeth so that we can use it as a calibration lock.
  • Merged the Ring with the blocker to be one big component.
  • Levelled the base that the ring sits on and made holes for sensor wires and water tubes to go through.
  • and of course, you guessed it, etc.
